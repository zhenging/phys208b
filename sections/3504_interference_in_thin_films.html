Thin-film Interference and Phase Shifts during reflection

Suppose a light wave with electric-field amplitude EiE_iEi​ is traveling in an optical material with index of refraction nan_ana​. It strikes, at normal incidence, an interface with another optical material with index nbn_bnb​. The amplitude ErE_rEr​ of the wave reflected from the interface is given by

Er=na−nbna+nbEiNormal incidence(35.16)\begin{aligned} E_r = \frac{n_a - n_b}{n_a+n_b}E_i \quad \text{Normal incidence} \quad \bold{(35.16)} \end{aligned} Er​=na​+nb​na​−nb​​Ei​Normal incidence(35.16)​

If the film has thickness t, the light is at normal incidence and has wavelength λ\lambdaλ in the film; if neither or both of the reflected waves from the two surfaces have a half-cycle reflection phase shift, the conditions for constructive and destructive interference are
Constructive reflection

2t=mλ(m=0,1,2,⋯)(35.17a)\begin{aligned} 2t = m\lambda \quad (m=0, 1, 2, \cdots) \quad \bold{(35.17a)} \end{aligned} 2t=mλ(m=0,1,2,⋯)(35.17a)​

Destructive reflection

2t=(m+12)λ(m=0,1,2,⋯)(35.17b)\begin{aligned} 2t = (m + \frac{1}{2}) \lambda \quad (m=0, 1, 2, \cdots) \quad \bold{(35.17b)} \end{aligned} 2t=(m+21​)λ(m=0,1,2,⋯)(35.17b)​

If one of the two waves has a half-cycle reflection phase shift, the conditions for constructive and destructive interference are reversed:
Constructive reflection

2t=(m+12)λ(m=0,1,2,⋯)(35.18b)\begin{aligned} 2t = (m + \frac{1}{2}) \lambda \quad (m=0, 1, 2, \cdots) \quad \bold{(35.18b)} \end{aligned} 2t=(m+21​)λ(m=0,1,2,⋯)(35.18b)​

Destructive reflection

2t=mλ(m=0,1,2,⋯)(35.18a)\begin{aligned} 2t = m\lambda \quad (m=0, 1, 2, \cdots) \quad \bold{(35.18a)} \end{aligned} 2t=mλ(m=0,1,2,⋯)(35.18a)​

Examples

Suppose the two glass plates in Fig. 35.12 are two microscope slides 10.0 cm long. At one end they are in contact; at the other end they are separated by a piece of paper 0.0200 mm thick. What is the spacing of the interference fringes seen by reflection? Is the fringe at the line of contact bright or dark? Assume monochromatic light with a wavelength in air of λ=λ0=500\lambda = \lambda_0 = 500λ=λ0​=500 nm.

Solution

Identify and Set Up: Figure 35.15 depicts the situation. We'll consider only the interference between the light reflected from the upper and lower surface of the air wedge between the microscope slids. [The top slide has a relatively great thickness, about 1 mm, so we can ignore interference between the light reflected from its upper and lower surfaces (see Fig. 35.14b)]. Light travels more slowly in the glass of the slides than it does in a air. Hence the wave reflected from the upper surface of the air wedge has no phase shift, while the wave reflected from the lower surface has a half-cycle phase shift (see Fig. 35.13c)
Execute: Since only one of the reflected waves undergoes a phase shift, the condition for destructive interference (a dark fringe) is Eq. (35.18b):

2t=mλ(m=0,1,2,⋯)\begin{aligned} 2t = m \lambda \quad (m=0, 1, 2, \cdots) \end{aligned} 2t=mλ(m=0,1,2,⋯)​

From similar triangles in Fig. 35.15 the thickness t of the air wedge at each point is proportional to the distance x from the line of contact.

tx=hl\begin{aligned} \frac{t}{x} = \frac{h}{l} \end{aligned} xt​=lh​​

Combining this with Eq. (35.18b), we find

2xhl=mλ0x=mlλ02h=m(1.25) mm\begin{aligned} \frac{2xh}{l} &= m \lambda_0\\ x &= m \frac{l \lambda_0}{2h} = m(1.25) \text{ mm} \end{aligned} l2xh​x​=mλ0​=m2hlλ0​​=m(1.25) mm​

Successive dark fringes, corresponding to m=1,2,3⋯m=1, 2, 3 \cdotsm=1,2,3⋯ are spaced 1.25 mm apart. Substituting m=0m=0m=0 into this equation gives x=0x=0x=0, which is where two slides touch (at the left-hand side of Fig. 35.15). Hence this a dark fringe at the line of contact.
