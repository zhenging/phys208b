2202 Calculating Electric Flux

Flux of a Uniform Electric Field

The electric flux through a flat area AAA perpendicular to a uniform electric field E⃗\vec{E}E is the product of the field magnitude EEE and the area AAA:

ΦE=EA\begin{aligned} \Phi_E = EA \end{aligned} ΦE​=EA​

In terms of vector area A⃗\vec{A}A perpendicular to the area, we can write the electric flux as the scalar product of E⃗\vec{E}E and A⃗\vec{A}A

ΦE=E⃗⋅A⃗(22.3)\begin{aligned} \Phi_E = \vec{E} \cdot \vec{A} \quad \bold{(22.3)} \end{aligned} ΦE​=E⋅A(22.3)​

EXAMPLE 22.1 ELETRIC FLUX THROUGH A DISK

A disk of radius 0.10 m is oriented with its normal unit vector n^\hat nn^ at 30°\degree° to a uniform electric field E⃗\vec{E}E of magnitude 2.0×103N/C2.0\times 10^3 N/C2.0×103N/C (Fig. 22.7). (Since this isn’t a closed surface, it has no “inside”
or “outside.” That’s why we have to specify the direction of n^\hat nn^ in the figure.)
(a) What is the electric flux through the disk?
(b) What is the flux through the disk if it is turned so that n^\hat nn^ is perpendicula to E⃗\vec{E}E?
(c) What is the flux through the disk if n^\hat nn^ is parallel to E⃗\vec{E}E?

Solution

a. The area A=π(0.10m)2=0.0314m2A = \pi(0.10m)^2 = 0.0314 m^2A=π(0.10m)2=0.0314m2, and the angle between E⃗\vec{E}E and A⃗=An^\vec{A} = A\hat nA=An^ is ϕ=30°\phi = 30\degreeϕ=30°, so from Eq. (22.1),

ΦE=EAcos⁡ϕ=(2.0×103N/C)(0.0314m2)(cos⁡30°)=54N⋅m2/C\begin{aligned} \Phi_E &= EA\cos\phi = (2.0 \times 10^3 N/C) (0.0314 m^2)(\cos 30\degree)\\ &= 54 N\cdot m^2/C \end{aligned} ΦE​​=EAcosϕ=(2.0×103N/C)(0.0314m2)(cos30°)=54N⋅m2/C​

b. THe normal to the disk is now perpendiculaa to E⃗\vec{E}E, so ϕ=90°\phi = 90 \degreeϕ=90°, and ΦE=0\Phi_E = 0ΦE​=0.
c. THe normal to the disk is parallel to E⃗\vec{E}E, so ϕ=0\phi = 0ϕ=0 and cos⁡ϕ=1\cos \phi = 1cosϕ=1:

ΦE=EAcos⁡ϕ=(2.0×103N/C)(0.0314m2)(1)=63N⋅m2/C\begin{aligned} \Phi_E &= EA\cos\phi = (2.0 \times 10^3 N/C) (0.0314 m^2)(1)\\ &= 63 N\cdot m^2/C \end{aligned} ΦE​​=EAcosϕ=(2.0×103N/C)(0.0314m2)(1)=63N⋅m2/C​

EXAMPLE 22.2 ELETRIC FLUX THROUGH A SPHERE

Solution

The surface is not flat and the electric field is not uniform, so to calculate the electric flux we must use the general definition, Eq. (22.5). Because the sphere is centered on the point charge, at any point on the spherical surface, E⃗\vec{E}E is directed out of the sphere perpendicular to the surface. The positive direction for both n^\hat nn^ and E⊥E_\perpE⊥​ is outward, so E⊥=EE_\perp = EE⊥​=E, and the flux through a surface element dAdAdA is E⃗⋅A⃗=EdA\vec{E} \cdot \d\vec{A} = E dAE⋅A=EdA.
