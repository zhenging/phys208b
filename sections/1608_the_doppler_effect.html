1608 The Doppler Effect

When a car approaches you with its horn sounding, the pitch seems to drop as the car passes. This phenomenon, first described by the 19th-century Austrian scientist Christian Doppler, is called the Doppler effect.

Moving Listener and Moving Source

Dopper effect for moving listener LLL and moving source SSS:

fL=v+vLv+vSfS(16.29)\begin{aligned} f_L = \frac{v + v_L}{v + v_S} f_S \quad \bold{(16.29)} \end{aligned} fL​=v+vS​v+vL​​fS​(16.29)​

fLf_LfL​ - Frequency heard by listener
fSf_SfS​ - Frequency emitted by source
vLv_LvL​ - Velocity of listener ( +++ if from LLL toward SSS, −-− if opposite)
vSv_SvS​ - Velocity of source ( +++ if from LLL toward SSS, −-− if opposite)

16.18 The police car is moving toward a warehouse at 303030 m/s. What frequency does the driver hear reflected from the warehouse?

Solution

This situation has two Doppler shifts (Fig. 16.34). In the first shift, the warehouse is the stationary “listener.” The frequency of sound reaching the warehouse, which we call fWf_WfW​, is greater than 300 Hz because the source is approaching. In the second shift, the warehouse acts as a source of sound with frequency fWf_WfW​ , and the listener is the driver of the police car; she hears a frequency greater than fWf_WfW​ because she is approaching the source.
The frequency reaching the warehouse is

fW=vv+vSfS=340340+(−30)(300)=329 Hz\begin{aligned} f_W = \frac{v}{v + v_S} f_S = \frac{340}{340 + (-30)} (300) = 329 \text{ Hz} \end{aligned} fW​=v+vS​v​fS​=340+(−30)340​(300)=329 Hz​

Then the frequency heard by the driver is

fL=v+vLvfW=340+30340)(300)=358 Hz\begin{aligned} f_L = \frac{v + v_L}{v} f_W = \frac{340 + 30}{340)} (300) = 358 \text{ Hz} \end{aligned} fL​=vv+vL​​fW​=340)340+30​(300)=358 Hz​
