1503 Mathematical Description of a Wave

Wave Function

Waves on a string are transverse; during wave motion a particle with equilirum position xxx is displaced some distance yyy in the direction perpendicular to the x-axis. The value of yyy depends on which particle we are takling about (xxx), and also on the time ttt. Thus yyy is a function of both xxx and ttt.

y=y(x,t)\begin{aligned} y = y(x, t) \end{aligned} y=y(x,t)​

Wave Function for a Sinusoidal Wave

Suppose that the displacement of a particle at the left end of the string (x=0x=0x=0), where the wave originates, is given by

y(x=0,t)=Acos⁡(ωt)=Acos⁡(2πft)(15.2)\begin{aligned} y(x=0, t) = A\cos(\omega t) = A \cos(2\pi f t) \quad \bold{(15.2)} \end{aligned} y(x=0,t)=Acos(ωt)=Acos(2πft)(15.2)​

The wave disturbance traves from x=0x=0x=0 to some point x to the right of the origin in an mount of time given by xv\dfrac{x}{v}vx​, where vvv is the wave speed. So the motion of point xxx at time ttt is the same at the motion of point x=0x=0x=0 at the earlier time (t−xv)(t-\dfrac{x}{v})(t−vx​). Hence we can find the displacement of point xxx at time ttt by simpley replacing ttt in Eq. (15.2) by (t−xv)(t-\dfrac{x}{v})(t−vx​).

y(x,t)=Acos⁡[ω(t−xv)]y(x, t) = A\cos\lb \omega (t - \frac{x}{v})\rb y(x,t)=Acos[ω(t−vx​)]

Because cos⁡θ=cos⁡(−θ)\cos \th = \cos(-\th)cosθ=cos(−θ), we can rewrite the wave function as

y(x,t)=Acos⁡[ω(xv−t)](15.3)y(x, t) = A\cos\lb \omega (\frac{x}{v} - t)\rb \quad \bold{(15.3)} y(x,t)=Acos[ω(vx​−t)](15.3)

Express Eq. (15.3) in terms of period T=1/fT=1/fT=1/f and the wavenlength λ=v/f=2πvω\lambda = v/f = \dfrac{2\pi v}{\omega}λ=v/f=ω2πv​:

y(x,t)=Acos⁡[2π(xλ−tT)](15.4)y(x, t) = A\cos\lb 2\pi (\frac{x}{\lambda} - \frac{t}{T})\rb \quad \bold{(15.4)} y(x,t)=Acos[2π(λx​−Tt​)](15.4)

Let k=2πλk = \dfrac{2\pi}{\lambda}k=λ2π​ (called wave number), we have λ=2πk\lambda = 2\pi kλ=2πk. We can then rewrite Eq. (15.4) as

y(x,t)=Acos⁡(kx−ωt)(15.7)\begin{aligned} y(x, t) = A\cos(kx - \omega t) \end{aligned} \quad \bold{(15.7)} y(x,t)=Acos(kx−ωt)​(15.7)

More on the Wave Function

For a wave travelling in the negative x-direction,

y(x,t)=Acos⁡[ω(xv+t)]=Acos⁡[2π(xλ+tT)]=Acos⁡(kx+ωt)(15.8)\begin{aligned} y(x, t) = A\cos\lb \omega (\frac{x}{v} + t)\rb = A\cos\lb 2\pi (\frac{x}{\lambda} + \frac{t}{T})\rb = A\cos(kx + \omega t) \quad \bold{(15.8)} \end{aligned} y(x,t)=Acos[ω(vx​+t)]=Acos[2π(λx​+Tt​)]=Acos(kx+ωt)(15.8)​

Particial Velocity and Acceleration in a Sinusoidal Wave

From the wave equation we can get an expression for the transverse velocity of any particle in a transverse wave. We can this vyv_yvy​ to distinguish it from the wave propagation speed vvv. To find the transverse velocity vyv_yvy​ at a particular point xxx, we take the derivative of wave function y(x,t)y(x, t)y(x,t) with respect to ttt, keeping xxx constant. If the wave function is

y(x,t)=Acos⁡(kx−ωt)y(x, t) = A\cos(kx - \omega t) y(x,t)=Acos(kx−ωt)

then

vy(x,t)=∂y(x,t)∂t=ωAsin⁡(kx−ωt)(15.9)v_y(x, t) = \frac{\partial y(x, t)}{\partial t} = \omega A \sin(kx - \omega t) \quad \bold{(15.9)} vy​(x,t)=∂t∂y(x,t)​=ωAsin(kx−ωt)(15.9)

Equation (15.9) shows that the transverse velocity of a particle varies with time, as we expect for SHM. The maximum particle speed is ωA\omega AωA; this can be greater than, less, or equal to the wave speed vvv, depending on the amplitude and frequency of the wave.
The acceleration of any particle is the second partial derivative of y(x,t)y(x, t)y(x,t) with respect to ttt

ay(x,t)=∂2y(x,y)∂t2=−ω2Acos⁡(kx−ωt)=−ω2y(x,t)(15.10)\begin{aligned} a_y(x, t) = \frac{\partial^2 y(x, y)}{\partial t^2} &=-\omega^2 A \cos(kx - \omega t)\\ &= -\omega^2 y(x, t) &\bold{(15.10)} \end{aligned} ay​(x,t)=∂t2∂2y(x,y)​​=−ω2Acos(kx−ωt)=−ω2y(x,t)​(15.10)​

We can also compute partial derivative of y(x,t)y(x, t)y(x,t) with respect to xxx, holding ttt constant. The first derivative ∂y(x,t)∂x\frac{\partial y(x, t)}{\partial x}∂x∂y(x,t)​ is the slope of the string at point xxx and at time ttt. The second partial derivative with respect to xxx tells us the curvature of the string.

∂2y(x,y)∂x2=−k2Acos⁡(kx−ωt)=−k2y(x,t)(15.11)\frac{\partial^2 y(x, y)}{\partial x^2} = -k^2 A \cos(kx - \omega t) = -k^2 y(x, t) \quad\bold{(15.11)} ∂x2∂2y(x,y)​=−k2Acos(kx−ωt)=−k2y(x,t)(15.11)

From Eqs. (15.10) and (15.11) and the relationship ω=vk\omega = vkω=vk we see that

∂2y(x,t)/∂t2∂2y(x,t)/∂x2=ω2k2=v2\frac{\partial^2 y(x, t) / \partial t^2}{\partial^2 y(x, t) / \partial x^2} = \frac{\omega^2}{k^2} = v^2 ∂2y(x,t)/∂x2∂2y(x,t)/∂t2​=k2ω2​=v2

and

∂2y(x,t)∂x2=1v2∂2y(x,t)∂t2(15.12)\begin{aligned} \frac{\partial^2 y(x, t)}{\partial x^2} = \frac{1}{v^2} \frac{\partial^2 y(x, t)}{\partial t^2} \quad \bold{(15.12)} \end{aligned} ∂x2∂2y(x,t)​=v21​∂t2∂2y(x,t)​(15.12)​
