Constructive and destructive two-Slit Interference

Constructive interference, two slits

dsin⁡θ=mλ(m=0,±1,±2⋯)(35.4)\begin{aligned} d\sin\th = m \lambda \quad (m=0, \pm 1, \pm 2 \cdots) \quad \bold{(35.4)} \end{aligned} dsinθ=mλ(m=0,±1,±2⋯)(35.4)​

ddd - Distance between slits
θ\thθ - Angle of line from slits to mth bright region on screen

Destructive interference, two slits

dsin⁡θ=(m+12)λ(m=0,±1,±2⋯)(35.5)\begin{aligned} d\sin\th = (m + \frac{1}{2}) \lambda \quad (m=0, \pm 1, \pm 2 \cdots) \quad \bold{(35.5)} \end{aligned} dsinθ=(m+21​)λ(m=0,±1,±2⋯)(35.5)​

ddd - Distance between slits
θ\thθ - Angle of line from slits to mth dark region on screen

Let ymy_mym​ be the distance from the center of the patter (θ=0\th = 0θ=0) to the center of the mth bright band. Let θm\th_mθm​ be the corresponding values of θ\thθ; then

ym=Rtan⁡θm\begin{aligned} y_m = R \tan \th_m \end{aligned} ym​=Rtanθm​​

The distance ymy_mym​ are often much smaller then the distance R from the slit to the screen. Hence θm\th_mθm​ is very small, then tan⁡θm≈sin⁡θm\tan \th_m \approx \sin \th_mtanθm​≈sinθm​. Combining this with Eq. (35.4), we find that for small angles only

ym=Rtan⁡θm≈Rsin⁡θm=Rmλd(m=0,±1,±2⋯)(35.6)\begin{aligned} y_m = R \tan \th_m \approx R \sin \th_m = R \frac{m \lambda}{d} \quad (m=0, \pm 1, \pm 2 \cdots) \quad \bold{(35.6)} \end{aligned} ym​=Rtanθm​≈Rsinθm​=Rdmλ​(m=0,±1,±2⋯)(35.6)​

35.59 Interference occurs with not only light waves but also all frequencies of electromagnetic waves and all other types of waves, such as sound and water waves. Suppose that your physics professor sets up two sound speakers in the front of your classroom and uses an electronic oscillator to produce sound waves of a single frequency. When she turns the oscillator on (take this to be its original setting), you and many students hear a loud tone while other students hear nothing. (The speed of sound in air is 340 m/s.) She then slowly moves one speaker away from you until it reaches a point at which you can no longer hear the tone. If she has moved the speaker by 0.34 m (farther from you), what is the frequency of the tone?

Solution

When you no longer hear the tone, destructive interference of sound waves occurs at your point. The path difference between two sound source is Δx=0.34m\Delta x = 0.34\text{m}Δx=0.34m. With destructive Interference, we have

Δx=(m+12)λ(m=0,±1,±2,⋯)λ=vf⇒f=mvΔx=.5×3400.34=500Hz(m=0)\begin{aligned} \Delta x &= (m + \frac{1}{2}) \lambda \quad (m=0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \cdots)\\ \lambda &= \frac{v}{f}\\ \To f &= \frac{mv}{\Delta x} = \frac{.5 \times 340}{0.34}\\ &= 500\text{Hz} \quad(m=0) \end{aligned} Δxλ⇒f​=(m+21​)λ(m=0,±1,±2,⋯)=fv​=Δxmv​=0.34.5×340​=500Hz(m=0)​
